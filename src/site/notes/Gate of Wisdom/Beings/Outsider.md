# Define

- Hebrew: zar (זָר) — "stranger, outsider, unauthorized one." 
	- At the time of writing, this word did not primarily mean a foreigner by nationality but one who stood outside a designated boundary of service — specifically anyone not of the tribe of Levi, and not consecrated, who attempted to approach or perform duties belonging to the Tabernacle. The Hebrew mindset framed holiness spatially and relationally: nearness to the Mishkan (Tabernacle) was reserved for those set apart by God, and a zar was anyone encroaching on that reserved space without authorization — regardless of whether they were an Israelite or not.
- Greek (LXX): allogenēs (ἀλλογενής) — "of another race/kind, alien." A term emphasizing "otherness of kind," reinforcing the idea of one who does not belong to the consecrated category, rather than a strictly ethnic foreigner.

# Related Words

- ger (גֵּר) — [[Gate of Wisdom/Beings/Stranger\|Stranger]], Sojourner, resident alien — distinct from zar; a ger is a welcomed outsider integrated into Israel's community (Exodus 22:21; Leviticus 19:34), while a zar is barred specifically from sacred service.
- zur (זוּר) — verb root, "to turn aside, be estranged, become foreign" (Psalm 78:30; Isaiah 1:4).
- nekar (נֵכָר) — [[Gate of Wisdom/Beings/Foreigner\|Foreigner]] "foreign, foreignness" — used of foreign gods and foreign peoples (Deuteronomy 32:12; Genesis 35:2), broader ethnic sense compared to zar's functional/sacred sense.
- eish zarah (אֵשׁ זָרָה) — "strange fire" (Leviticus 10:1) — the same root applied to unauthorized worship practice.
- toshav (תּוֹשָׁב) - [[Gate of Wisdom/Beings/Sojourner\|Sojourner]] temporary resident, sojourner, settler, inhabitant. Often times appears as the Ger Toshav.   The lived AMONG the Israelites, inside the camp or town. Non-Israelite living in the land. Accepted basic moral standards. Not fully converted.

# Additional Information

- Numbers 1:51 sets the pattern: "the stranger who comes near shall be put to death" — repeated verbatim in Numbers 3:10, 3:38, and 18:7, all concerning unauthorized approach to the Tabernacle by anyone other than Aaron and his sons.
- The same root describes Nadab and Abihu offering "strange fire" (eish zarah) in Leviticus 10:1, resulting in their deaths — the clearest narrative illustration of what "zar" encroachment costs.
- Numbers 16:40 explicitly recalls this principle after Korah's rebellion, using zar to describe "anyone who is not a descendant of Aaron."
- Outside the cultic context, zar is applied to the adulteress/forbidden woman in Proverbs 5:3, 5:20, and 7:5 — one who is "outside" the bounds of covenant marriage, showing the word's consistent sense of illegitimate nearness/access.
- Hosea 5:7 uses zar of illegitimate children, "strange children," born outside covenant faithfulness.

## Personal Notes

[[Sacred Truces/Foreigner, Sojourner, Stranger Among You\|Foreigner, Sojourner, Stranger Among You]]
- In regard to the Tabernacle, the dwelling place of Yah, if an outsider or unauthorized person even approached or came near the border, they were put to death. (Numbers 1, 3, 18).
- There aren’t verses about the Outsider or someone who is just passing through, even just through the outer edges of the camp. There were strict boundaries around the camp or city, and an unpermitted person would be stopped. Every single person had to have permission to enter the camp. If they did not have permission to be there, you were driven out.
- Justice was not allowed to be perverted or altered just because of someone’s status of any kind. If someone broke a law, they weren’t given any special privilege because they were a passing through or a temporary resident. They also could not be treated unjustly. Yah is very clear when applying the law, there is to be no partiality to the poor or the great. (Isaiah 1, Deut 16, James 2). Even though the foreigner has no intention of staying in the community, while there, they were required to keep every law as if they were native-born. This meant, if they broke the law, they would face the same penalties which included being put out of the community, barred from return, and even death. (Exod 12; Lev 20, 24; Deut 17). Once inside the land, no one could openly defy Yah god’s law or treat set apart (holy) things as common.
