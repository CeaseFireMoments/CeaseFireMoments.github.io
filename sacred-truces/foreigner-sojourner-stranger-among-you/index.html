Foreigner, Sojourner, Stranger Among You

As we as Yah (God) followers, we can not pluck a verse or two out of the bible and use it to support our own beliefs. We need to sit with the verse, consider the people, the cultures, and ultimately what Yah says elsewhere in order to get the full depth of that verse. Then, maybe it does support our own beliefs, or may it will force us to examine our beliefs. We may be forced to choose between tov (good) and ra (bad).

This is the very decision “Adam” and “Eve” had to make. If we pay attention to what Genesis says, “Out of the ground Yahweh God made every tree to grow that is pleasant to the sight, and good for food, including the tree of life in the middle of the garden and the tree of the knowledge of good and evil.” EVERY tree was pleasant to the sight and good for food – EVERY. In case you didn’t get what EVERY meant, the writer points out – it included BOTH the tree of life AND the tree of the knowledge of tov and ra.

What separated the tree of the knowledge of tov and ra from all the others? Yah said … that is as simple as it gets. Your belief could be pleasant to the sign and good. But if Yah said something different, then you will be forced to choose between your belief and walking with him. Because the moment you choose what you believe over what Yah said … you have eaten from the tree of the knowledge of tov and ra and you are no longer in the garden.

Not everyone is actually equal to Yah, and he makes that very clear over and over. Let’s talk about the categories he gives to people in regard to their living status and treatment.

Outsider – Zar Undocumented / Illegal Alien / Illegal Immigrant

In regard to the Tabernacle, the dwelling place of Yah, if an outsider or unauthorized person even approached or came near the border, they were put to death. (Numbers 1, 3, 18).

There aren’t verses about the Outsider or someone who is just passing through, even just through the outer edges of the camp. There were strict boundaries around the camp or city, and an unpermitted person would be stopped. Every single person had to have permission to enter the camp. If they did not have permission to be there, you were driven out.

Justice was not allowed to be perverted or altered just because of someone’s status of any kind. If someone broke a law, they weren’t given any special privilege because they were a passing through or a temporary resident. They also could not be treated unjustly. Yah is very clear when applying the law, there is to be no partiality to the poor or the great. (Isaiah 1, Deut 16, James 2). Even though the foreigner has no intention of staying in the community, while there, they were required to keep every law as if they were native-born. This meant, if they broke the law, they would face the same penalties which included being put out of the community, barred from return, and even death. (Exod 12; Lev 20, 24; Deut 17). Once inside the land, no one could openly defy Yah god’s law or treat set apart (holy) things as common.

Foreigner – Nokhri/Ben Nekhar – Tourist

Using the WEB version of the Bible, this would appear as foreigner. In the New testament the term stranger and foreigner. See Matthew 25:35, Ephesians 2:19, Hebrews 11:13.

The Foreigner was someone who lived OUTSIDE the Israelites camp. There “permanent resident” was in their own nations which were not in the immediate area of the Israelites. They could remain only temporarily and had very limited rights. Think of these people as those here on vacation or on a B1 or B2 visa. They were a temporary or non-resident alien.

These people were allowed to participate in a temporary manner such as going to the market and eat things the Israelites could not eat. The Israelites could lend to them with interest. Although Yah provides general blessings to all people, the foreigner was not entitled to the covenantal blessings. (Deut 14, 23)

They were subject to the general “moral” laws of the land. The foreigner was prohibited in taking part in certain feasts and some rituals unless they decided to convert which would move them into the Sojourner status. (Exod 12)

The foreigner wasn’t entitled to the same gleaning and community support as those who lived among the Israelites. Israelites could sell certain items to them. They did not fall under the debt remission which was offered to others. (Deut 14, 15)

The Canaanite woman whom Jesus addresses in Mark 7, would have fallen into this category. This woman has a young daughter who had an unclean spirit. She comes to Jesus and falls at his feet and asked him to cast the unclean spirit out of her daughter. And he said, ““Let the children be filled first, for it is not appropriate to take the children’s bread and throw it to the dogs.” The children are the people of Israel – the native-born. The Israelites had been instructed to displace the Canaanites (Deut 7, 20, 23). Her faith moved her from this status, and Jesus honored that.

Jesus made a prioritizing of the “lost sheep of Israel” but also responded with compassion and healing for those who came to him in faith.

Sojourner – Toshav – Visa, Refugee, Asylee

In English translations you might see “Non-Israelite living in the land,” “temporary resident”, “resident alien”, “foreigner”, “sojourner”. See passages such as Exodus 12:14, 45; Leviticus 25:6, 23. It is often pared with Ger to become “stranger and sojourner”. This is translated as alien, sojourner, foreigner, exile in the New Testament, see Acts 7:9, 1 Peter 2:11, Ephesians 2:19.

These people live WITHIN the land of Israel, but were less integrated. They can legally live and work in the community but have limited benefits. They might be a hired worker, tenant, or short-term settler. Their stay intent is more on a temporary basis. They accept the “moral” laws and reside under the Israelite authorities. They were a resident alien in more of an economic way.

A Toshav is an uncircumcised non-Israelite living in the land. Once circumcised, he becomes a native-born. The distinction between a Toshav and a Ger Toshav isn’t always easy to determine and are used interchangeably. The difference is a Toshav is more temporary where a Ger Toshav is more permanent.

The Roman Centurion would almost fit this category because he was living WITHIN the land of Israel. He asked Jesus for his servant to be healed. Jesus recognized his faith and healed his servant.

Stranger Foreigner – Ger Toshav – Green Card Holders

Using the WEB version of the bible, this usually appears as “stranger who lives as a foreigner”, “resident alien”, “foreigner who resides among you”. In others you might see “resident alien” or Sojourner”. See passages such as Leviticus 19:33-34.

The sojourner was someone lived AMONG the Israelites, inside the camp or town. They were a non-Israelite living in the land, pitching their tents among the tents of the Israelites. Their intent is to stay long term or permanently. Accepted basic “moral” standards. Not fully converted. They weren’t integrated into the community and weren’t necessarily interested in becoming a member of the community. Think of these people as being those in a country on a green card status. They have been given permission to live and work freely, legal protections, access to many services, but not full citizen.

They agreed to follow the key laws in order to be allowed to live peacefully among the Israelites. But they did not fully convert to becoming an Israelite. They kept their own identity and didn’t swear allegiance to Israel. Later, this meant they would generally follow the Noahide laws.

The Israelites were forbidden to mistreat or oppress these people. They could be given food and clothing as they were seen as vulnerable. This meant they were allowed to glean the fields, vineyards, and crops along with the native orphans and widows. The native-born would harvest their crops but were instructed not to do the corners and they couldn’t go back for a second time to get what was missed. THEN the sojourner, poor, orphans, and widows could go out and pick what was left. In addition, every third year people brought 10% of “everything in the land, whether grain or fruit because it belongs to the Lord” to be stored up. This was meant to provide for the Levites, the poor, orphans, widows, and sojourners. It was a community welfare system. They could also receive produce from the Sabbath year (Lev 25, Lev 27, Num 18)

Some of the “mixed multitude” that came out of Egypt would have been in this category. The woman at the well is a pretty good example of this category.

A Toshav is an uncircumcised non-Israelite living in the land. Once circumcised, he becomes a native-born

Converted Stranger – Ger – Naturalized Citizen

In English this appears as “”as one of you”, “as the native among you” or just “stranger” who then qualifies for full rights. See passages such as Exodus 12:48. This usually appears as proselyte, stranger, sojourner, and foreigner in the New Testament. See Mathew 23:15, Acts 2:10, 6:5, 13:43.

These people lived fully AMONG the Israelites. Originally they were a part of the “mixed multitude” that came out of Egypt. They have full rights, nearly identical to native-born citizens. They fully participated in the feasts and tithes. They could for the most part own property and as poor, they were treated similar to an Israelite as far as support. (Lev 19, Exod 12, 20, Deut 14)

These people received the same benefits, privileges, love and status as a native born Israelite. They are under the exact same law as the Israelite. All the other status have exceptions and restricted from participation in some feasts and rituals. (Exod 12, Lev 24, Num 15)

A ger who fully converts — circumcised (if male), accepts all the Torah commandments, and joins the covenant people are treated as a native-born Israelite in almost every way. After this conversion, they would simply be referred to as a native-born.

As a native-born he had rights to almost anything a true native-born had rights to, except Priesthood and Kingship, and some tribal land inheritances.

Caleb would have been considered a Ger, he was a Kenizzite, was not a blood born Israelite but this is rarely known because he is known as one of the leaders of the Israelites.

Native-born – Ezrach – Citizen

In English this appears as “native-born”, “homeborn,” and “native of the land”. Someone born into the people of Israel.

There were two ways to be deemed native-born:

  • Through the father’s line. So if the father was descendant from Jacob/Israel, he was a native born.
  • Two former Gers have a child. Their children after that would be considered full Israelite by birth. Except Priesthood because that came from the line of Aaron.
    • Rahab, a Canaanite, and her family
      • Ruth (a Moabite)

These people receive full inheritance of the land, full participation in rituals and feasts. If from Aaron’s line they could participate in serving in the Tabernacle/Temple. Could become King. They are a part of the covenant through birth and have automatic inclusion in the inheritance promised. (Exod 12, Deut 17, Lev 24)

Gentile and Nation

Gentile:

  • A non-Jewish person – Someone who does not follow Judaism. It can even mean someone who is a descendant of Judah – but not be a part of Judaism. Who is saying the word gentile goes a long way in determining the meaning.
  • A non-Jewish person – Someone who is not from the people of Israel (the descendants of Jacob/Israel).
  • Anyone outside of Israel (including Romans, Greeks, Syrians, etc.).
  • “Pagans” or “heathens”

nation:

  • All the peoples of the world outside of Israel
  • “Pagan nations” or “the heathen.”
  • Sometimes simply means “all nations” (including Israel)

If you actually read all this, the first question to ask yourself is – which of these am I in God’s Kingdom? How am I living? Everyone of these types of people believed in YHWH – or we could say today would have believed in Jesus, the difference is how they responded to that belief. How committed they were to the ways of living in that Kingdom they were.

  • Am I living like a Foreigner? A temporary resident, with my permanent residence somewhere else? Not fully submitted to the ways of God? Still making determination between tov and ra based on my own determination, or old ways, or outside influences?
  • Am I living like a Sojourner? A more permanent resident accepting the “moral” laws but not the others?
  • Am I living like a Stranger Foreigner? Agreeing to follow the key laws in order to be allowed to live peacefully in the kingdom. Not fully convert to becoming a citizen? Keeping your own identity Generally following the Noahide laws?
  • Am I living like a Converted Stranger? Lived fully AMONG the Israelites, fully participated in the feasts, accepting all the Torah commandments?
  • Am I living like a Native-Born? Has my heart been truly circumcised? Have I allowed “the Lord your God will circumcise your hearts and the hearts of your descendants, so that you may love him with all your heart and with all your soul, and live?” Have I allowed Yah god to “put my law [Torah] in their minds and write it on their hearts.” (Deut 30, Jer 31) Do I “keep the laws of God and have the testimony of Jesus?”

The other question is, how do my beliefs about citizenship line up with Yah god’s? Are you going to eat the fruit of the tree of knowledge of tov and ra – despite what God said?
