Terms

These words emphasize themes of haste (no time for dough to rise during the Exodus), purity (no fermentation/souring), simplicity/humility (plain bread vs. puffed-up leavened bread), and remembrance of deliverance from Egypt. In Scripture, eating matzah is both a command and a symbol of sincerity and holiness.

The main Hebrew word for unleavened bread (or unleavened cakes)

  • matstsâh or matzah / matzo (מַצָּה)
    • Pronunciation: mats-tsaw' (often pronounced "mat-zah" in modern Hebrew)
    • It specifically refers to bread or cake made without leaven (yeast or any rising agent).
    • Literally derived from the root מָצַץ (māṣaṣ, H4711), which carries the idea of "greedily devouring for sweetness" or "sucking out."
      • Thus, it properly means "sweetness" — in the sense of something not soured or bittered by fermentation/yeast. It is "sweet" because it remains plain and unfermented.
    • In context, it is an unfermented cake or loaf, and can also elliptically refer to the Feast of Passover/Unleavened Bread (because no leaven was used).
      • This is the word translated as "unleavened bread" or "unleavened cakes" throughout the Old Testament passages you listed earlier (e.g., Exodus 12:8, 12:15, Leviticus 2:4, Deuteronomy 16:3, etc.).
      • Plural form: מַצּוֹת (matzot).

The general Hebrew word for bread

  • lechem (לֶחֶם)
    • Pronunciation: lekh-em
    • The ordinary word for bread, food made from grain (usually wheat or barley).
    • It can refer to leavened or unleavened bread depending on context.
    • Broader meaning includes food in general (e.g., "bread of affliction" in Deuteronomy 16:3 is lechem oni, where oni means affliction, poverty, or oppression — referring to the simple, hasty bread eaten during the Exodus).
      • In the context of unleavened bread, the Bible often combines it with matzah, such as "unleavened bread" (matzot) or simply calls the specific item matzah while using lechem for the concept of bread. No single standalone Hebrew adjective for "unleavened" exists apart from matzah; the concept is conveyed directly by the noun matzah (meaning bread/cake that is unleavened).

Related terms

  • chametz or hametz (חָמֵץ) — the opposite of unleavened: leavened bread or anything fermented/soured. It symbolizes corruption or sin in many biblical interpretations.

In the New Testament (Greek), the equivalent is ἄζυμος (azymos), meaning "unleavened," which appears in passages like 1 Corinthians 5:7-8 ("unleavened bread of sincerity and truth").

Days

There is a first and last day for this Feast. It lasts for seven (07) days.

The Feast of Unleavened Bread is a seven-day observance instituted by God as a perpetual statute for Israel, directly tied to the Exodus from Egypt. It begins on the 15th day of the first month (the day after the Passover on the 14th) and ends on the 21st day. The Scriptures describe its first day and its seventh (last) day as special “holy convocations” (days of sacred assembly with no regular work allowed), while the entire period requires eating only unleavened bread and removing all leaven from the homes. No passage explains a symbolic or hidden reason for choosing exactly seven days or for designating only the first and last days as convocations beyond the fact that God commanded it this way as a memorial of deliverance.

  • Exodus 12:15-20 (the original institution on the night of the Exodus)
  • Leviticus 23:5-8 (the formal calendar of feasts)
  • Numbers 28:16-25 and Deuteronomy 16:1-8 repeat the same seven-day structure
